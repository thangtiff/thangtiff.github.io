Problem:

Remotely Operated Vehicles (ROVs) are tethered, teleoperated underwater robots used for marine research and in the maintainence of offshore oil and gas rigs in place of human divers in dangerous or difficult situations. Operating ROVs can be challenging, as operators will need to rely on data from its cameras and sensors to provide information about the subsea environment. Distortion effects and lack of depth cues can make performing subsea work with an ROV difficult.



Proposed Solution:

In collaboration with the Monterey Bay Aquarium Research Institute (MBARI) and the Olin School of Engineering, I worked with marine biologists, ROV pilots, UX researchers and programmers in evaluating the usability of a VR application to help pilots operate underwater remotely operated vehicles (ROVs) for scientific marine research. Our focus was on evaluating the differences in usability, cognitive load and task performance when comparing existing ROV teleoperation formats with 2D monitors to a monosocpic and stereoscopic ROV VR teleoperation interface.

As a UX Researcher for this project, I ...


  • Conducted a literature review regarding the usability and human factors issues in ROV operation and the use of VR technologies in piloting ROVs
  • Designed an empirical user study evaluating usability, task performance, presence and cognitive load of operating an ROV using exisiting controls versus monoscopic and stereoscopic VR interfaces for ROV operation
  • Ran user study with 12 participants using the MBARI test tank, and collected data using the NASA TLX, System Usability Scale and Slater-Usoh-Steed Presence Questionnaire
  • Wrote and submitted manuscript detailing user study and findings to ACM VRST 2021, winning the "Best Paper" award
