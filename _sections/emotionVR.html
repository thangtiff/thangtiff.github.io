Role:


UX Researcher, Game Developer

Problem:


  • Individuals with developmental disabilities face challenges in identifying emotions through emotional cues, such as facial features, vocal intonations and body language

  • Being able to recognize emotions is a cruicial part of developing relationships with others, often playing a key role in maintaining friendships, romantic relationships and professional relationships

Solution:


  • VR can provide a safe, controlled and realistic method of social skills training where individuals with developmental disabilities can practice recognizing and responding to emotional cues

  • In collaboration with non-profit organization, Hope Services, I developed a detective-themed VR game, EmotionVR, to assist adults with developmental disabilities in identifying emotions through collective facial features


Methods:


  • Conducted usability tests with the HTC Vive VR headset with adults with developmental disabilities, identifying pain points and design accommodations

  • Interviewed users on their experiences with using VR and identifying emotions in social scenarios, using data to design game content

  • Developed high-fidelity prototypes using Unity to conduct user tests with, evaluating the impact and feasibility of the game for social skills training

Results and Takeaways:


  • Adults with developmental disabilities enjoy using VR as a social skills training tool, but more realistic avatars are necessary to improve emotion recognition outcomes

  • Work presented at UC Santa Cruz 2018 Grad Slam competition

  • Findings published and presented at the 2018 International Conference on Disability, Virtual Reality and Associated Technologies (ICDVRAT '18) and IEEE International Conference on Smart Computing (SMARTCOMP '18)
